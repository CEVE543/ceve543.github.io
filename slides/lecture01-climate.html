What questions will we answer?

Most of the exmaples I‚Äôll give are related to water or electricity, but they apply to other types of risks as well

Sizing a stormwater pipe

  1. Rainfall-runoff model
    • e.g., peak flow from rational method: \(Q = CiA\)
    • \(i\) is rainfall intensity, \(A\) is area, and \(C\) is runoff coefficient
  2. Design rainfall based on return period \(T\)
    • \(p(i > i^*) = 1/T\)
  3. Size your culvert to handle \(Q^* = Ci^*A\)
  4. Requires knowing \(p(i)\)!

Floodplain mapping in a riverine system

This is a moderately simplified workflow

  1. Analyze historical streamflow data at a gauge
  2. Take the 99th percentile (100 year return level) of annual maximum streamflows
  3. Use a hydraulic model to model where the water goes

Reservoir sizing (simplest version)

  1. Consider \(N\) years of inflows (and releases, evaporation, etc)
  2. Count number of times reservoir is empty (‚Äúfailure‚Äù)
  3. Repeat experiment many different times with different inflows
  4. If you are sampling this from \(p(\text{inflow})\), you can estimate the reliability
    1. Monte Carlo method
    2. Why not just use observed inflows?

Index insurance pricing

  1. Index insurance: if some index \(I\) is above a threshold \(I^*\), pay out \(X\)
    • Total rainfall in a season, area flooded, etc
  2. Let \(p^* = p(I > I^*)\) is the probability of a payout
  3. Naive pricing: \(R = p^* X\)
  4. Risk premium: \(R = X \left( \mathbb{E}[p^*] + \lambda \mathbb{V}^{1/2}[p^*] \right)\)
